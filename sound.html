Vibration made visible.

Sound is nothing but matter oscillating. Strike something — it pushes air molecules, which push the next ones, which eventually push your eardrum. No matter, no sound. Interact with each demo below.

Vibrating Objects Produce Sound

Every sound begins with something moving back and forth. A tuning fork's prongs vibrate hundreds of times per second, pushing and pulling the air around them into a wave.

Strike it and watch the wave fronts spread outward. The wave dies away as the fork loses energy.

Sound Through Particles

Sound is a longitudinal wave — particles oscillate in the same direction as the wave travels. They don't travel themselves; they just nudge their neighbours.

Amplitude and Frequency

Amplitude is the size of each oscillation — a bigger amplitude means louder sound. Frequency is how many oscillations per second — higher frequency means a higher pitch.

04 — Reflection
move the wall · shout

Echo requires ≥ 17 m

Echoes

An echo is a reflected sound that arrives at least 0.1 s after the original — your ear can then perceive it separately. For that, the reflecting surface must be at least 17 metres away (sound travels 340 m/s × 0.1 s ÷ 2).

Closer than 17 m → the reflected wave merges back and you hear only a slight "thickening" of the original sound, not a distinct echo.

Outer → Middle → Inner → Brain

How the Human Ear Hears

Sound funnels through the pinna → bounces off the eardrum → three tiny bones (ossicles) amplify it → the cochlea converts vibration to electrical signals → the auditory nerve carries them to the brain.
