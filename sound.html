Topic 02

Sound

Sound is vibration — a to-and-fro motion pushing on the air around it, one particle nudging the next, until it reaches your ear. No medium, no sound. Tap through the simulations below.

01 — Source of sound

Vibrating objects produce sound

Every sound starts with something moving back and forth, fast. Pluck the tuning fork or the drum skin and watch it vibrate.

Try it

Click the stage to strike the tuning fork. Notice the ripples spreading outward as it vibrates — that's the sound wave being born.

02 — Propagation

Sound traveling through particles

Sound moves as a compression wave — particles bunch up (compression) then spread out (rarefaction), passing the disturbance along without any particle actually traveling far.

Try it

Watch one highlighted particle — it only jiggles in place, even as the wave pattern races across the box.

03 — Loudness and pitch

Amplitude and frequency

Amplitude is how big the vibration is — it controls loudness. Frequency is how fast it repeats — it controls pitch.

Try it

Drag both sliders and watch — and if your device has sound on, listen to the pitch and volume change live.

04 — Reflection

Echoes

An echo is just sound bouncing off a distant, hard surface and arriving back late enough for your ear to hear it as a separate sound — the surface needs to be at least ~17 m away.

Try it

Set the wall distance and shout. Move the wall closer than 17 m and the echo merges into the original sound instead of arriving as a separate one.

05 — Hearing

How the human ear receives sound

Sound funnels down the ear canal, vibrates the eardrum, gets amplified by three tiny bones, and turns into nerve signals in the cochlea.

Try it

Send a sound wave in and follow it stage by stage: outer ear → eardrum → ossicles → cochlea → brain.
